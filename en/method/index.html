How a folder of 30 prompts is built

The number is not the organising idea. The genre boundary is. Each folder begins with one sound that can be described clearly and varied without turning into a different product.

1. Fix the core sound

Rhythm family, low-end behaviour, source material and spatial character define the centre of a collection. Trap Ritual keeps trap drums and a dominant 808; Garaje Oscuro keeps human 2-step swing; Fossil Jungle keeps resampled breaks and heavy sub-bass. The same rule applies to the other three folders.

2. Choose the variation axes

The 30 prompts do not repeat one template with synonyms. They move concrete controls: drum pocket, bass movement, sample family, motif, silence, width, dirt or room. The relevant axes change from genre to genre.

3. Write complete directions

Each entry stands on its own. Buyers choose one complete prompt; they are not asked to combine fragments, codes or numbered modules. The title identifies the entry, but the working text remains self-contained.

4. Keep exclusions separate

The 10 negative prompts live in their own PDF because they are optional. Each one addresses a specific boundary, such as unwanted vocals or an unsuitable finish. They do not replace the main direction.

5. Explain the files in two languages

The ES and EN folders contain equivalent documentation in their own language. The prompts themselves remain in English in both. The quick guide explains where each text goes, how to work with or without vocals and what the relevant controls do.

6. Include four audio references

Every product lists four MP3 references in the download and exposes one 30-second public preview. The reference profile is now written on each product page, so the sound can be evaluated before checkout without revealing the paid prompt.
